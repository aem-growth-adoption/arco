Your first espresso shot

Pull this recipe on your first day with the Primo + Macinino. It's a calibration shot — not meant to be perfect, but to tell you which way to adjust.

Equipment
  • Arco Primo (warmed up for 15 minutes)
  • Arco Macinino grinder
  • Precision scale (0.1g)
  • Shot glasses or small cup
  • Fresh beans (4–14 days post-roast)
  1. Flush the group head for 3 seconds to stabilise temperature.
  2. Set the Macinino to a medium-fine grind (start at position 4).
  3. Weigh 18g of coffee into the portafilter basket.
  4. Distribute evenly, then tamp with firm, level pressure.
  5. Lock the portafilter in and start the shot immediately.
  6. Aim for 36g yield in 25–30 seconds. Stop the shot at 36g.
  7. Taste. Too sour and fast? Grind finer. Too bitter and slow? Grind coarser.
Tips
  • Don't dial in with a brand-new bag — let beans degas for at least 4 days after roast.
  • Change only one variable (grind size) between shots until you're in range.
  • A naked portafilter reveals channelling — worth the upgrade once you're dialled in.

Getting started: what to expect in week one

The first week with a new espresso setup is a calibration exercise. Your goal is not to pull the perfect shot — it's to understand your grinder's range and how your beans behave. Expect to adjust grind size with every new bag and after the first 20 shots with a fresh bag as CO₂ degases.

Water quality matters more than most beginners expect. If your tap water tastes fine, it'll probably work. If it tastes of chlorine or lime, use filtered water. A TDS of 75–150 ppm is ideal for espresso extraction.

Keep a simple log for the first two weeks: dose in, yield out, time, and a tasting note (sour / balanced / bitter). Three data points like that and you'll understand your setup faster than any guide can teach you.

Verdict: who should pick which

Choose the Primo if you primarily drink espresso or americanos, make one or two drinks at a time, have a compact kitchen, or are buying your first quality machine. The $700 difference buys a lot of great coffee. The Primo's sequential workflow becomes natural within a week.

Choose the Doppio if you regularly steam milk for lattes or flat whites, often serve two or more people at once, or find yourself waiting for the machine to recover between shots. The dual-boiler architecture eliminates that friction completely — you can pull a shot and steam milk simultaneously with no temperature recovery time.

If you're on the fence, the Primo is the safer starting point. You can always upgrade to the Doppio later; you'll understand exactly what you're buying when you do.

How many coffee drinks do I make each morning?

If it's 1–2 drinks for yourself: any tier works well. If it's 3–5 drinks for a household: the Primo handles this comfortably if drinks are spaced slightly. If it's 4+ milk drinks back-to-back without waiting: you want a dual-boiler machine (Doppio or above) for simultaneous steaming.

Do I mostly drink espresso or milk drinks (lattes, flat whites)?

Mostly espresso and americanos: any machine in the lineup will excel. Milk drinks are the bigger differentiator — the Primo makes excellent milk drinks sequentially; the Doppio and above steam while you pull the shot, which matters when you're making multiple drinks in a rush.

How important is heat-up speed?

The Nano heats in 45 seconds — ideal if you forget to turn it on until you need it. The Primo takes 90 seconds. The Doppio takes 3 minutes but rewards you with better thermal stability once it's up to temperature. If your routine involves leaving the machine on a timer, heat-up time matters less.

Will I want to upgrade in two or three years?

If you're buying your first quality machine and aren't sure how deep into espresso you'll go: start with the Primo. It has a high ceiling — you can improve your technique for years before the machine becomes the limiting factor. Upgrading from Primo to Doppio is a meaningful step with clear, feelable differences.

What budget should I set aside for a grinder?

Budget the same as your machine, or roughly half at minimum. A good grinder has more impact on shot quality than a machine upgrade. The Macinino ($549) pairs perfectly with the Nano and Primo. The Macinino Pro ($799) is the right match for the Doppio and Studio. Don't buy a $900 machine with a $100 grinder.

What to expect when you get started

Every espresso machine has a learning curve. The first week is mostly about grind size — that's the variable with the most impact on shot quality, and dialling it in takes a handful of shots per new bean bag. Expect this and enjoy the process. A shot pulled 10% too fast is still better than a pod machine.

Grind fresh. Pre-ground espresso loses flavour within minutes. A flat-burr grinder like the Macinino will have more impact on your shot quality than any machine upgrade. It's not optional — it's the foundation. Budget at least half the price of your machine for a grinder.

Water quality matters. If your tap water tastes clean, it'll work. If you're in a hard-water area, a simple filter or bottled mineral water (TDS 75–150 ppm) will improve both the taste and the longevity of your machine. Descale every 2–3 months.

Your first shot: the calibration recipe

Pull this on day one. It's not meant to be perfect — it's meant to show you which direction to adjust.

  1. Warm up your machine for 15 minutes before the first shot of the day.
  2. Flush the group head for 3 seconds to stabilise temperature.
  3. Grind 18g of coffee at a medium-fine setting into your portafilter.
  4. Tamp with firm, level pressure — about 15 kg. Keep the tamp straight.
  5. Lock the portafilter in and start the shot. Watch the drip.
  6. Stop at 36g of liquid in the cup. Note the time — target is 25–30 seconds.
Tips
  • Shot too fast, tastes sour? Grind finer. One notch at a time.
  • Shot too slow, tastes bitter? Grind coarser. One notch at a time.
  • Shot tastes good but ratio is off? Adjust dose (in) or yield (out), not grind.
  • Once in range on one bag, expect to re-dial for every fresh bag — beans change.
